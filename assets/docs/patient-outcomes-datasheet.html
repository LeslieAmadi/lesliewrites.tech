Patient Outcomes Datasheet

Before a model is trustworthy, its data must be accountable. This is a Datasheet for Datasets for an anonymised, multi-centre healthcare dataset documenting where the data came from, how it was de-identified, what it may and may not be used for, and the bias and fairness risks anyone building on it must confront. It's the governance artifact that makes a clinical ML dataset safe to use.

Motivation & the problem

A research team wants to train a model to flag patients at risk of a serious chronic condition. The data exists spread across seven hospitals' electronic health records but using it raises immediate questions: Where exactly did it come from? Was it properly de-identified? Which patients are missing from it? What is it allowed to be used for? Without those answers written down, every team re-asks them, and worse, some don't ask at all.

A datasheet is accountability for data

The Patient Outcomes Dataset exists to support early detection and monitoring of chronic illness using anonymised EHR data. This datasheet is what makes that use safe: it records the data's origin, governance, de-identification, intended and forbidden uses, and known biases so a model built on it can be fair, transparent, and traceable, and a governance board can sign off knowing exactly what they're approving.

Longitudinal records from adult patients across a mixed urban–suburban network of seven centres.

Patients~520,000 unique anonymised records
Institutions7 medical centres (acute care & outpatient)
GeographySingle country; urban, peri-urban, and suburban mix
Record typesDemographics, vitals, medical history, lab tests, lifestyle indicators

Demographicsage_band, sex, region, insurance_typeHighly aggregated to reduce re-identification risk

Lab resultscreatinine, hba1c, egfr, ldlNormalised units; extreme outliers clipped
Medical historyDiagnosis codes, comorbidities, prior admissionsCoded to a shared standard across sites
Split by patient, never by encounter

The dataset is split by patient 70% train / 15% validation / 15% test not by encounter. This is a subtle but critical governance detail: if one patient's visits landed in both train and test, the model would “remember” them and report inflated scores. Documenting the patient-level split protects every downstream evaluation from that leakage.

Collection & de-identification pipeline

The most governance-critical part of the datasheet is how raw hospital data becomes a safe research asset. Identifiers are removed at the source institution, before data ever reaches the central environment the single most important safeguard in the whole pipeline.

The pipeline. Direct identifiers are stripped at each hospital before transfer; only de-identified, highly aggregated data crosses into the secure, audited research enclave, where it's standardised, cleaned, and split by patient into the ML-ready dataset.

Governance behind the data

ApprovalSigned off by each centre's internal data governance board.
Permitted useResearch or ML evaluation under an approved protocol only.
Re-identificationExplicitly prohibited in all usage agreements.
PHI / PIIDirect identifiers removed at source; only aggregated attributes retained.

Intended & out-of-scope uses

The pairing of what the data is for and what it must never be used for is the ethical core of the datasheet.

Intended uses
  • Training models for early detection of chronic conditions.
  • Developing readmission-risk and triage-prioritisation tools.
  • Benchmarking model performance across demographic subgroups.
  • Research into fair, transparent clinical ML.
Out-of-scope uses
  • Direct individual diagnosis or real-time treatment decisions.
  • Sole evidence in clinical or legal decision-making.
  • Insurance pricing, eligibility, or coverage scoring.
  • Any attempt to infer identity or re-identify a patient.
🧑‍⚕️

Bias, fairness & ethics

A datasheet that hides its dataset's weaknesses is worse than none, because it lends false confidence. This section names the biases plainly so every downstream team confronts them.

Known riskWhy it matters
Demographic underrepresentationSome age groups and regions are thin models may be less reliable for them.
Healthcare-access biasReflects only people who reached the healthcare system, not the general population.
Incomplete social determinantsIncome, environment, and other context are largely absent, limiting causal claims.
⚠ Required fairness checks for any model built on this data

The datasheet doesn't just warn it prescribes. Any model must evaluate performance separately across sex, age bands, and regional groups; monitor calibration curves per subgroup; and document any performance trade-off introduced by a fairness constraint. Naming the checks turns “be fair” from an aspiration into a checklist.

Ethical use guidance

Models derived from this dataset must undergo external validation and local clinical review before any real-world use. The data is one input into a broader clinical and ethical evaluation, never the sole basis for a decision about a patient.

Maintenance & versioning

AspectPolicy
Refresh cadenceMajor refresh annually; minor quality fixes as patch versions.
DeprecationDeprecated records are flagged, not deleted to preserve auditability.
ReproducibilityConsumers record the dataset version alongside each training and evaluation run.
Version-stamp every run

The datasheet asks every consumer to log the dataset version with their model runs. It's a small discipline that makes results reproducible and lets anyone trace exactly which slice of data a given model saw essential when a clinical model's behaviour is ever questioned.

Key learnings

Document the gaps, not just the contents.

The most valuable section is the one naming who's underrepresented a datasheet that hides weaknesses lends false confidence.

Split by patient is a governance decision.

A single line split by patient, not encounter protects every downstream evaluation from leakage.

Prescribe, don't just warn.

Turning “be fair” into a named list of required subgroup checks is what makes the ethics section actionable.

📝 Writer's note

A datasheet is one of the purest tests of technical writing in a governance context: it must be precise enough for a data scientist, clear enough for a clinician, and complete enough for a governance board all at once. I structured it on the established Datasheet-for-Datasets framework so it reads as a recognised, auditable artifact rather than an ad-hoc readme.

The sections I weighted most heavily were de-identification, out-of-scope uses, and bias because in healthcare ML, the harm comes not from what the data does but from what people assume it can do. Stating the limits as firmly as the capabilities is the difference between a dataset that's merely available and one that's genuinely safe to build on.
