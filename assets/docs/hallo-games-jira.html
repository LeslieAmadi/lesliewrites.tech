Hallo Games Ticket Workflow

A ticket workflow is a contract between teams: it defines not just the steps a piece of work moves through, but who can move it, what has to be true first, and crucially which moves are forbidden. I documented the end-to-end Jira workflow for partner integrations across five teams, with entry/exit criteria, SLAs, a RACI, and the transition rules that keep every ticket auditable.

The business problem

Partner integrations cross five teams Customer Success, QA, Legal, Product, and the Head of CS who coordinates it all. Without a shared workflow, a ticket means different things to different people: CS thinks it's ready for QA, QA thinks it's still with the customer, and a ticket can quietly slide backward from “Ready to Go Live” to “Customer Integration” with no record of why. The result is stalled integrations, finger-pointing, and an audit trail full of holes.

A workflow is a contract, not a suggestion

The fix isn't a prettier board it's a documented contract: every status has a precise meaning, every transition has an owner and a precondition, and some transitions are explicitly forbidden to protect the audit trail. This document is that contract, so a ticket's position always means the same thing to all five teams.

The workflow

Every partner integration moves through seven states in a single forward path. The diagram shows the happy path along the top and, below it, the moves that are explicitly forbidden because a workflow is defined as much by what you can't do as by what you can.

The workflow as a state machine: a single forward path from To Do to Live (top, then down and back along the second row). The two forbidden transitions below are blocked deliberately any post-release work or rework opens a new, traceable ticket rather than silently moving an existing one backward.

Each status carries a meaning and explicit entry/exit criteria, so there's no ambiguity about when a ticket has earned its place.

Transition rules

The transitions are where the workflow becomes governance. Each allowed move has an owner and a condition; the forbidden moves are listed explicitly with the reason, because that's the part most workflows leave dangerously unsaid.

Allowed transitions
To Do → Client SetupHOCS
Client Setup → Pending FeedbackCS / HOCS
Customer Integration → QA TestingCS
QA Testing → Ready to Go LiveQA
Ready to Go Live → LiveHOCS / CS
Forbidden transitions
Live → QA TestingPost-release tests = new ticket
Ready to Go Live → Customer IntegrationBackward move breaks audit trail
Why forbidding a transition is the most senior decision here

It's easy to document the steps work flows through. The harder, more valuable judgment is deciding which moves must never happen because a ticket silently sliding backward destroys the record of what actually occurred. Forcing rework into a new, linked ticket keeps every state a ticket ever held permanently true. That's the difference between a board and a governed workflow.

⏱️

Key learnings

Forbidden transitions are the real design work.

Defining which moves are impossible protected the audit trail more than any amount of detail on the allowed path.

Entry/exit criteria end the debates.

Once each status had explicit exit criteria, “is this ready for QA?” stopped being an opinion and became a check.

An SLA per status surfaces silent stalls.

Tickets no longer disappeared in “Pending Customer Feedback” the review cadence pulled them back into view.

📝 Writer's note

Most workflow documentation describes the happy path and stops. I treated this as a state machine, which forced the more valuable questions: which transitions are allowed, who owns each, and which must be forbidden to keep the record honest.

The forbidden-transition section is the part I'm proudest of, because it's the most senior judgment in the document. Anyone can list the steps; deciding that a ticket may never silently move backward and that rework must open a new, linked ticket instead is what turns a Jira board into a governed, auditable process the whole organisation can trust.
