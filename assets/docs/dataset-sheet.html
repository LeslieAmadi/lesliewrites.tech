1. Motivation

The Customer Support Chatbot Corpus was created to support the development of a multilingual, production-grade customer service assistant capable of understanding and responding to common product enquiries.

The dataset enables training and fine-tuning of conversational models in English and German, particularly for e-commerce and technical support environments.

2. Composition

  • Data Type: Text conversations (user messages + agent responses)
  • Total Size: 120,000 dialogue pairs
  • Languages: English (70%), German (30%)
  • Domains: E-commerce, technical troubleshooting, FAQs
  • Dialogue Length: 1–6 messages per exchange

2.1 Example Data Structure

{
  "user": "Hi, where is my order?",
  "agent": "Your order is currently being processed. Delivery is expected on Thursday."
}

3. Collection Process

Data was compiled from anonymised customer service chat logs contributed by partner companies. All text was manually cleaned and checked by trained annotators to remove noise, formatting issues, and incomplete interactions.

3.1 Privacy & Anonymisation

  • Names, emails, phone numbers, and addresses removed via automated redaction.
  • Annotated review ensured no personal identifiers remained.
  • Data collected and processed under GDPR-compliant workflows.

3.2 Annotation

  • Language labelling (EN/DE)
  • Topic categorisation (shipping, returns, troubleshooting, etc.)
  • Quality grading (low-quality entries removed)

4. Recommended Uses

  • Training intent recognition or classification models
  • Fine-tuning LLMs for customer service tone and clarity
  • Evaluation benchmarks for multilingual chatbot performance

5. Not Recommended For

  • Sentiment analysis — text is largely neutral and templated
  • Legal, medical, or high-risk chatbots — domain is not relevant
  • Toxicity detection — minimal conflict or abusive content included

6. Ethical Considerations

Because the dataset reflects real customer interactions, certain linguistic or stylistic biases may be present. The tone leans toward polite, formal service language, which may influence models trained on it.

6.1 Known Ethical Risks

  • Bias toward service-oriented phrasing, reducing stylistic diversity.
  • Potential under-representation of edge cases or uncommon query types.
  • Synthetic politeness may shape overly compliant model behaviour.

6.2 Mitigations

  • Additional datasets recommended to rebalance tone.
  • Model output monitoring for hallucinations or over-apologising behaviours.
  • Continuous dataset expansion for under-represented topics.
