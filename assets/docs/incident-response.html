Incident summary

On 14 February 2025, the NimbusTech Payment Processing Service (NPPS) suffered severe latency spikes followed by intermittent failures. Around 31% of customer transactions failed or timed out during the window. The trigger was a database connection-pool exhaustion that followed a routine infrastructure scaling event.

The incident lasted 2 hours 36 minutes and was classified Severity 1 (Critical) for its customer impact, financial transaction failures, and direct disruption to partner APIs.

Why this report matters as much as the fix

The outage was resolved in under three hours. The report exists to ensure it never happens again and that the response is defensible to partners and regulators. A good post-incident report is a blameless record that turns one bad day into permanent organisational learning that's what this document is built to do.

Root cause analysis

The surface trigger was connection-pool exhaustion. But a good RCA doesn't stop at the symptom — it keeps asking why until it reaches something a process change can fix. The 5-Whys chain walks from the visible failure down to the real, systemic cause.

The 5-Whys chain. Each answer becomes the next question, walking from the visible symptom down to a systemic root cause: a change-management gap that let a scaling policy ship without SRE review. Fixing the symptom wouldn't prevent recurrence; fixing the process does.

Stop at the cause you can fix with a process, not a person

The discipline of the 5 Whys is to keep going past “someone misconfigured the autoscaler” which invites blame and fixes nothing to “our change process allowed an unreviewed scaling change.” The second is blameless, systemic, and actionable. That shift is what separates a report that prevents recurrence from one that just assigns fault.

Key learnings

The first hypothesis is often wrong.

An hour went to chasing an external-partner theory. A good report records the wrong lead too it's where the lesson about faster diagnosis lives.

Drive the RCA to a process, not a person.

The root cause wasn't a bad config it was a review gap that let the config ship. That's the only level at which prevention is real.

A resolution isn't done until it's validated.

Monitoring, QA, and customer confirmation together turn “it looks fine now” into a defensible close.

📝 Writer's note

The hardest and most valuable part of an incident report is the root-cause analysis, because it's where an organisation decides whether to blame a person or fix a system. I structured the RCA explicitly as a 5-Whys chain so the document walks the reader from symptom to systemic cause, rather than just asserting one.

I also deliberately kept the wrong first hypothesis in the timeline. A sanitised report that pretends the team went straight to the answer teaches nothing; the hour spent on a false lead is exactly where the lesson about detection and diagnosis lives. Honesty is what makes a post-incident report blameless and useful instead of defensive.
