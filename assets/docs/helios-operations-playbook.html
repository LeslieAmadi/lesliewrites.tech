Disaster recovery — objectives

Disaster recovery answers two questions with numbers, not adjectives: how much data can we lose (RPO) and how fast must we be back (RTO). Every DR procedure on this page is built to hit the targets below. Vague promises like "highly available" are deliberately absent.

Disaster scenarios & response

Three classes of disaster, each with a documented detection signal and response.

ScenarioDetectionResponseTarget
Single-region outageRegion health checks fail; error rate spikes in one zoneRouter fails traffic over to nearest healthy region automaticallyRTO < 15 min
Registry corruption / lossRegistry integrity check fails or registry unreachableRestore from daily backup; replay artifact sync log since last snapshotRPO < 5 min
Full platform outageMulti-region failure; control plane unreachableInvoke cross-cloud standby; restore registry; bring regions up in priority orderRTO < 60 min
Executable runbook regional failover (SEV-1)
SeveritySEV-1 — region down, user-facing impact
Symptoms
  • Region health checks failing for > 2 minutes.
  • Error rate in the affected region > 50%.
  • Latency in-region climbing without recovery.
Verify
shell · confirm the region is genuinely down
kubectl --context eu-west get nodes        # is the region reachable at all?
promtool query instant 'up{region="eu-west"}' # health signal
# confirm it is not a monitoring false positive before failing over
Fail over
shell · shift traffic to healthy region
helios-ctl failover --from eu-west --to eu-central
helios-ctl traffic verify --region eu-central  # confirm uptake
# watch Grafana "Regional Traffic" until eu-central absorbs the load
Success criteria
  • Error rate back under 0.5% within 15 minutes (RTO met).
  • No data loss beyond the 5-minute RPO window.
  • Failed region drained, not serving partial traffic.
  • Incident logged; recovery of original region scheduled separately.
⚠ DR is only real if it is rehearsed

A disaster recovery plan that has never been executed is a hypothesis. The platform team runs a scheduled "game day" each quarter: a region is deliberately failed in a controlled window and the failover runbook is executed end to end against the clock. The RTO and RPO numbers on this page are measured from those drills, not estimated.

Capacity planning

Capacity planning is the discipline of having enough headroom to absorb growth and spikes before they happen without paying for idle GPUs all year. Helios plans capacity on a rolling basis using three inputs: baseline demand, growth trend, and peak multiplier.

The headroom model

InputHow it is measuredPlanning use
Baseline demandMedian RPS over the trailing 30 days, per modelThe floor capacity must always cover
Growth trendMonth-over-month RPS growth rateProjects demand 1–2 quarters ahead
Peak multiplierRatio of P99 peak RPS to median (campaigns, seasonality)Sizes the burst headroom autoscaling must reach
Target utilisationThe 50–70% band from the SLO referenceKeeps pods efficient without removing burst room
The planning rule

Provision so that baseline demand × peak multiplier still lands inside the 50–70% utilisation band. If a model runs at 60% utilisation at median load with a 2× campaign multiplier, it has no burst headroom a campaign will saturate it. The capacity plan flags any model whose projected peak would exceed 85% utilisation and schedules additional capacity before the next quarter.

Capacity review cadence

ReviewFrequencyOwnerTrigger for action
Utilisation reviewWeeklyPlatform teamAny model sustained > 75% utilisation
Growth projectionMonthlyPlatform + ProductProjected peak > 85% next quarter
Pre-campaign checkBefore each launchProduct + PlatformExpected multiplier exceeds current headroom
⚠ The pre-campaign check

The campaign-spike incident on the main page is exactly what this check prevents. Before any marketing launch, the product team declares the expected traffic multiplier and the platform team confirms the headroom exists or provisions it. A documented check turns "we hope it scales" into "we verified it scales." This single procedure closes the loop on the opening scenario of the whole Helios documentation set.

📝 About this companion page

This operations playbook is the companion to the main Helios documentation. I split disaster recovery and capacity planning into their own page deliberately: the main architecture document should be readable in one sitting, and operational reference material is consulted at different moments during an incident, or during quarterly planning by different people.

That split is itself a documentation decision. A 12,000-word page that tries to be both an introduction and an operations manual serves neither purpose well. Two linked, focused documents one to understand the platform, one to operate it under pressure is how mature documentation estates are actually structured.
