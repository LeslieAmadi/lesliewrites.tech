Executive Summary

This case study documents the design, deployment, and governance of an AI-driven clinical decision support system implemented by a mid-sized healthcare provider. The platform augments clinician judgement by surfacing early-stage diagnostic risk indicators from longitudinal patient data.

As the senior technical writer on the initiative, I led the creation of end-to-end documentation covering system architecture, model behaviour, risk mitigation, and clinician-facing guidance—ensuring regulatory alignment, clinical trust, and long-term system maintainability.

Business & Clinical Context

The organisation faced increasing diagnostic complexity driven by rising patient volumes, fragmented historical records, and time-constrained clinical workflows. Leadership identified misdiagnosis risk and clinician burnout as strategic concerns.

  • Average clinician workload exceeded 120 patients per day
  • Early-stage indicators were frequently buried in longitudinal data
  • Diagnostic accuracy varied significantly across departments
  • Manual data synthesis limited time for patient interaction

Solution Overview

The solution introduced a supervised machine learning platform that produces interpretable risk scores to support—rather than replace—clinical decision making. The system was explicitly designed with human-in-the-loop safeguards and explainability as first-class requirements.

Model Strategy

  • Logistic Regression for baseline transparency and calibration
  • Random Forest for feature importance and robustness
  • Neural Networks for complex temporal pattern recognition

Outputs were normalised into a single risk score (0–1) accompanied by clinician-readable explanatory factors.

High-Level System Design

Patient Records
   ↓
Secure ETL Pipeline
   ↓
Feature Engineering Layer
   ↓
ML Inference Service
   ↓
Risk Score & Explainability API
   ↓
Electronic Medical Record (EMR)
   ↓
Clinician Review & Decision

All integrations complied with healthcare data protection requirements, with anonymisation and access controls enforced at every layer.

Governance, Risk & Compliance

  • Bias Management: Stratified sampling, demographic audits, SHAP-based reviews
  • Over-Reliance Risk: Mandatory clinician confirmation before action
  • Model Drift: Continuous monitoring with alert thresholds
  • Data Governance: Compliance-approved pipelines and audit trails

Documentation played a critical role in aligning engineering, compliance, and clinical leadership around acceptable risk thresholds and operational safeguards.

Outcomes & Measured Impact

  • Diagnostic accuracy: 91%
  • False negatives reduced: 23%
  • Hospital readmissions: 18% reduction
  • Clinician time saved: ~2.8 hours per week

Beyond quantitative gains, clinician trust increased significantly due to transparent documentation and explainability-first design.

Key Learnings

  • AI adoption succeeds when documentation addresses human concerns—not just systems
  • Explainability is a trust requirement, not a feature
  • Early documentation alignment accelerates regulatory approval
  • Senior technical writing functions as organisational risk management
