Product Requirements Document

A PRD is the contract between product, engineering, design, and compliance it states the problem, who it's for, exactly what we will build, and how we will know it worked. This one defines a Responsible Play Limits feature for a regulated iGaming platform, where every requirement carries both a product goal and a compliance obligation.

The opening problem

A regulated iGaming operator is preparing for a licensing review. The regulator requires that players can set their own deposit, loss, and session limits, that those limits take effect immediately, and that any increase is subject to a mandatory cooling-off period. Today the platform has none of this. Three teams each hold part of the answer product knows the player experience, engineering knows the wallet system, compliance knows the regulatory text and nobody has written down the single, shared definition of what "done" means.

Without one source of truth, engineering builds to its best guess, compliance discovers gaps in audit, and the feature ships late after rework. This PRD is that source of truth: it turns a regulatory obligation and a product idea into a precise, testable, buildable definition that every team agrees to before a line of code is written.

🔌 What a PRD is and is not

A PRD states what we are building and why, and how we'll know it works. It is not a design spec (that's how it looks) or a technical design (that's how it's built). It is the shared contract those documents are written against.

Goals & non-goals

Every PRD needs to say what success looks like and, just as important, what is deliberately out of scope so the build stays focused.

Goals (in scope)
  • Players can set deposit, loss, and session limits themselves, at any time.
  • Lowering a limit (more protective) takes effect immediately.
  • Raising a limit (less protective) is held for a cooling-off period before it applies.
  • Every limit change is logged with a tamper-evident audit trail.
  • Players are notified clearly whenever a limit changes or is about to change.
Non-goals (out of scope)
  • Operator-imposed limits (a separate risk-team feature).
  • Self-exclusion and account closure (handled by a distinct workflow).
  • Affordability checks based on external financial data.
  • Cross-operator limit sharing via a national register.
  • Marketing or bonus eligibility logic.
✅ Why non-goals matter

Naming what we are not building prevents scope creep and stops reviewers from blocking the PRD over concerns that belong to other features. It is one of the highest-leverage sections in the document.

Goals are only real if they're measurable. These are the signals that tell us the feature works for the player, the business, and the regulator.

⚠ The non-negotiable metrics

Two targets here are compliance gates, not aspirations: cooling-off enforcement and audit completeness must be 100%. A single miss is a regulatory finding, not a bug to prioritise later.

User stories

Requirements that aren't traceable to a user need are how products drift. Each story below is the "why" behind a cluster of requirements that follow.

US-1

As a player, I want to set a weekly deposit limit so that I can stay in control of what I spend.

US-2

As a player, I want a lower limit to take effect right away so that a decision to cut back isn't undone by a delay.

US-3

As a player, I want a clear explanation when raising a limit is delayed so that I understand the wait is a protection, not an error.

US-4

As a compliance officer, I want a complete, tamper-evident record of every limit change so that I can demonstrate enforcement in an audit.

US-5

As a support agent, I want to see a player's current limits and pending changes so that I can answer questions without altering anything I shouldn't.

Functional requirements

Each requirement has a unique ID (so tests, designs, and tickets can reference it), a priority, the story it serves, and a one-line rationale. IDs never get reused that's what makes traceability hold over time.

IDRequirementStoryPriority
FR-01A player can set and edit a deposit, loss, and session limit from account settings.US-1Must
FR-02Decreasing any limit applies immediately and blocks activity beyond the new value.US-2Must
FR-03Increasing any limit is queued and applies only after the cooling-off period elapses.US-3Must
FR-04A pending increase can be cancelled by the player before it takes effect.US-3Should
FR-05The player is notified on every change: applied, queued, or cancelled.US-3Must
FR-06Every limit event is written to an append-only audit log with actor, timestamp, and values.US-4Must
FR-07Support can view current limits and pending changes in read-only form.US-5Should
FR-08Limit settings are reachable within two taps from the main account screen.US-1Could
📑 MoSCoW priority

Must / Should / Could / Won't keeps the conversation honest about what ships first. Here, anything tied to immediate decrease, cooling-off, notification, and audit is a Must because each maps to a regulatory obligation below.

Compliance requirements

This is what makes a regulated-product PRD different from an ordinary one: each obligation from the regulatory text is restated as a requirement, mapped to the functional requirement that satisfies it, and tied to the evidence an auditor will ask for. No obligation is left implicit.

IDObligationSatisfied byEvidence
CR-01Players must be able to self-set limits.FR-01Settings UI + audit log
CR-02Protective changes must apply without delay.FR-02Timestamped apply event
CR-03Limit increases must be subject to a cooling-off period.FR-03Queued-change record + elapsed timer
CR-04All changes must be recorded and retained for the licensing period.FR-06Append-only audit export
CR-05Players must be informed of changes affecting their protection.FR-05Notification delivery log
🔗 Traceability is the deliverable

The value isn't the list it's the chain: obligation → requirement → test → evidence. When an auditor points at any rule, the team can walk the chain to a passing test and a stored record in seconds.

The limit-change decision

The single most important behaviour in this feature is how a limit change is handled: protective changes apply now, risky changes wait. A diagram removes all ambiguity about that branch.

The asymmetry is the whole point: the system always errs toward player protection. Anything that reduces risk is instant; anything that increases it must wait.

Acceptance criteria

"Done" has to be testable by someone who didn't write the requirement. These criteria are written so QA and compliance can both run them and agree on the result no interpretation required.

AC for FR-02 immediate decrease
  • Given a player has a weekly deposit limit of 100,
  • When they change it to 50,
  • Then the new limit of 50 is enforced on the very next deposit attempt, and the change appears in the audit log with a timestamp.
AC for FR-03 cooling-off on increase
  • Given a player has a weekly deposit limit of 50,
  • When they request an increase to 100,
  • Then the limit remains 50 until the cooling-off period elapses, the increase is shown as "pending," and the player can cancel it before it applies.
AC for FR-06 audit completeness
  • Given any limit change of any kind,
  • When the event occurs,
  • Then an append-only record exists with actor, old value, new value, timestamp, and outcome and it cannot be edited or deleted.

Release plan, risks & open questions

A PRD closes by saying how the work reaches players safely, what could go wrong, and what's still undecided so nothing important lives only in someone's head.

PhaseScopeGate to advance
Phase 1Decrease + audit log (the protective core)Compliance sign-off on audit completeness
Phase 2Increase + cooling-off + notificationsEnforcement verified at 100% in test
Phase 3Support read-only view + UX polishSupport trained; criteria all passing
Risks
  • Cooling-off timer logic is the highest-risk area an off-by-one here is a compliance miss.
  • Notification delivery failures could leave players uninformed of a change.
  • Wallet-system coupling: limits must hold even during deposit spikes.
Open questions
  • Exact cooling-off duration awaiting regulatory confirmation.
  • Should session limits warn before logout, or enforce hard?
  • Retention window for audit export confirm against licence terms.

Key learnings

💡 A requirement without an ID can't be tested or traced

The unglamorous discipline of unique, never-reused IDs is what lets a regulator point at a rule and the team walk it straight to a passing test. Traceability is a documentation job before it's an engineering one.

💡 Non-goals protect the build as much as goals direct it

Stating what's out of scope stopped reviewers from blocking the PRD over self-exclusion and affordability real concerns that simply belong to other features.

💡 In a regulated product, "what" and "must comply" are the same sentence

Writing the compliance obligations as requirements not as a separate appendix nobody reads is what kept this feature audit-ready from day one instead of after a finding.

✍️ Writer's note

The hardest part of a PRD like this isn't the writing it's getting product, engineering, and compliance to agree on one definition of "done" before the build starts. The document is just the place that agreement gets recorded. I treated every requirement as a small contract: specific enough to test, traceable enough to audit, and honest about what we were deliberately leaving out.
