Model Name: LexiMind Predictive Diagnosis Model (LPD-1.0)
Version: 1.0
Release Date: 9 November 2025
Domain: Chronic disease risk estimation
Summary. The LexiMind Predictive Diagnosis Model estimates the likelihood of selected chronic illnesses from structured patient data. It is designed as a clinical decision-support tool: it surfaces risk signals for clinicians and care teams, but it does not issue diagnoses or treatment plans.

1. Overview

LexiMind Predictive Diagnosis uses patient demographics, lab results, and lifestyle information to estimate risk for conditions such as diabetes, heart disease, and kidney disease. The model outputs probabilities that can support early screening, prioritisation, and follow-up planning.

The model is part of the broader LexiMind Health Intelligence family, which focuses on interpretable, clinician-centred AI for healthcare environments.

2. Intended Use & Users

2.1 Intended Use

  • Support clinicians in early screening for chronic diseases.
  • Highlight potentially high-risk patients for follow-up, additional tests, or lifestyle interventions.
  • Augment dashboards or risk registries with an additional predictive signal.

2.2 Intended Users

  • Hospital and clinic teams (doctors, nurses, care coordinators).
  • Healthcare product teams integrating risk scores into clinical software.
  • Data and analytics teams running risk stratification or population health reports.

2.3 Out-of-Scope Uses

  • Issuing fully automated diagnoses or treatment plans.
  • Insurance pricing, coverage decisions, or denial of care.
  • Use on paediatric populations or clinical domains not represented in training data.

3. How the Model Works

LPD-1.0 is an ensemble model that combines several algorithm families to balance performance, robustness, and interpretability:

  • Logistic Regression for well-calibrated baseline risk.
  • Random Forest for handling non-linear feature interactions and missingness patterns.
  • Neural Network for capturing more complex relationships in larger feature sets.

The ensemble aggregates outputs from each component model and applies calibration to produce final probability estimates per condition.

4. Training Data & Features

4.1 Data Sources (Conceptual)

  • De-identified electronic health record (EHR) datasets from partner hospitals.
  • Adult patient population (ages 18+), with recorded chronic disease outcomes.
  • Labels derived from diagnostic codes and clinician-confirmed case definitions.

4.2 Input Features

Example families of input features include:

  • Demographics: age, sex.
  • Vitals: blood pressure, BMI (derived), resting heart rate.
  • Lab results: fasting blood sugar, HbA1c (if available), cholesterol profile.
  • Lifestyle: smoking status, activity level (if available).
  • History: family history of hypertension, diabetes, heart disease, kidney disease.

Direct identifiers (names, addresses) are excluded from model training, focusing on clinical and lifestyle variables instead.

5.2 Outputs

For each patient, the model returns:

  • Risk probabilities for:
    • Diabetes
    • Heart disease
    • Kidney disease
  • An optional aggregated risk score or category (e.g. low / medium / high).

6. Performance (Illustrative)

The original high-level metric for this model was an overall accuracy of ~91%. To reflect realistic evaluation for healthcare models, LPD-1.0 is instead characterised using discrimination and calibration metrics.

6.2 Example Impact Metrics (Conceptual)

  • Reduction in hospital readmissions for targeted high-risk cohorts: ≈18% in offline simulations.
  • Estimated time saved per clinician: ~3 hours per 50 patients when integrated into workflows.

These impact numbers are illustrative and depend on how the model is integrated into real-world care pathways.

7. Calibration, Monitoring & Drift

Because the model outputs probabilities, calibration is important. LPD-1.0 uses post-hoc calibration techniques to align predicted risks with observed event rates in the development cohorts.

  • Calibration curves are monitored periodically on held-out evaluation data.
  • Input feature distributions are monitored for drift (e.g. shifts in age distribution or lab ranges).
  • Detected drift should trigger re-evaluation and potential retraining on more recent data.

8. Fairness, Limitations & Known Risks

8.1 Limitations

  • Trained on a specific adult population; performance may differ on new regions or health systems.
  • Quality of predictions depends on the quality and completeness of input data.
  • Does not account for all possible comorbidities, social determinants, or nuanced clinical context.

8.2 Potential Bias & Fairness Considerations

  • If certain demographic groups are under-represented in training data, performance may be lower in those groups.
  • For safe deployment, teams should evaluate performance on their own population, including key subgroups where possible.
  • Risk scores should never be used to deny care or justify reduced attention to any patient or community.

8.3 Known Risks

  • Over-reliance on model outputs without clinical judgement.
  • Misinterpretation of risk probabilities as deterministic outcomes.
  • Potential miscalibration when usage context diverges significantly from training conditions.

9. Responsible AI & Human Oversight

LPD-1.0 is designed to support, not replace, human expertise. Recommended patterns for responsible use:

  • Use the model as a second opinion signal, not the final decision.
  • Combine risk scores with clinical guidelines, patient preferences, and additional diagnostics.
  • Provide training and documentation to clinicians to interpret model outputs appropriately.

9.1 Data Privacy & Anonymisation

  • Data used for training and evaluation is de-identified where possible.
  • Access to raw training data is restricted to authorised teams under data governance controls.

9.2 Model Maintenance

  • Model performance and calibration should be re-evaluated periodically.
  • Retraining schedules should consider clinical practice changes and new data availability.
