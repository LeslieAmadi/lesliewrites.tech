Operational Playbook

The document an operations team actually opens — at the start of a shift, during a maintenance window, and at 3am when something breaks. This playbook defines how a service is run day to day: routine operations, on-call and escalation, severity levels, runbooks for known failures, and the handover that keeps operations continuous across shifts.

The business problem

A service runs fine until the one person who knows how it works is on holiday. Then a routine task becomes a guessing game, an alert fires and no one's sure who owns it, and a small issue escalates because the steps to fix it lived only in someone's memory. Operations that depend on heroes don't scale and don't sleep — and every shift handover is a chance for something to fall through.

A playbook turns expertise into a procedure anyone can run

The goal isn't to document everything — it's to make the service runnable by someone who isn't the expert. A good operational playbook captures the routine tasks, the known failures and their fixes, who to escalate to and when, and how one shift hands off cleanly to the next. It's the difference between operations that depend on people and operations that depend on a process.

Routine operations

Most of operations is the un-dramatic work that prevents drama. The playbook captures these as checklists tied to a cadence, so they happen regardless of who's on shift.

CadenceTasks
Start of shiftReview dashboards, check overnight alerts, read the handover note, confirm on-call coverage.
DailyVerify backups, check queue depths and error rates, clear expected warnings.
WeeklyReview capacity trends, test alerting, confirm runbooks match reality.
End of shiftWrite the handover note: open issues, watch-items, anything mid-flight.
🚨

Severity levels

Before anyone can respond proportionately, everyone has to agree what “serious” means. The severity scale is the shared language that sets response speed and who gets pulled in.

SeverityMeansResponse
Sev-1 · CriticalService down or unusable; data at riskImmediate; all-hands, escalate now
Sev-2 · MajorMajor feature broken; many users affectedUrgent; on-call + owning team
Sev-3 · MinorLimited impact; workaround existsSame day; on-call handles
Sev-4 · LowCosmetic or low-impactScheduled; normal backlog
⚠ Severity sets the response, not the panic

A shared severity scale stops two failure modes at once: treating a cosmetic bug like an outage, and under-reacting to a real one. Because the scale also maps to who gets pulled in, calling the severity correctly is the first and most important decision of any incident.

On-call & escalation path

When the on-call can't resolve something alone, the escalation path tells them exactly who's next — no guessing, no waking the wrong person. The diagram shows the ladder.

The escalation ladder. On-call (L1) attempts a runbook fix first; unresolved issues move to the owning team (L2), and a Sev-1/2 fast-tracks to an incident lead (L3) who coordinates the response and communications. Anything resolved is logged and closed.

🔨

Runbooks for known failures

The heart of the playbook: for each recurring failure, a runbook gives the symptom, the likely cause, and the exact steps to fix it — so the on-call doesn't reverse-engineer a solution at 3am. A sample entry:

Runbook — Queue backing up
Symptom

Processing queue depth rising steadily; jobs delayed beyond SLA.

Likely causes

A stuck worker, a downstream dependency slow or down, or a traffic spike beyond capacity.

Steps
  • Check worker health; restart any unresponsive workers.
  • Check the downstream dependency's status and latency.
  • If traffic-driven, scale workers up within the agreed limit.
  • If depth keeps rising after these steps, escalate to the owning team (L2).
Do not

Don't purge the queue to clear the alert — it discards real work. Escalate instead.

The “do not” line is as valuable as the steps

Under pressure, the tempting shortcut is often the dangerous one — purging a queue, forcing a restart, clearing state. Writing the explicit “do not” for each runbook prevents the well-meaning 3am mistake that turns an incident into a bigger one.

Maintenance windows

Planned work carries its own risk. The playbook makes maintenance deliberate and visible, never a surprise.

StepWhat happens
SchedulePick a low-traffic window; avoid freeze periods and other teams' releases.
AnnounceNotify internal teams and, if user-facing, customers in advance.
PrepareHave a rollback plan and a way to verify success before starting.
Execute & verifyDo the work, confirm health, then announce completion.
🔄

Shift handover

The riskiest moment in continuous operations is the gap between shifts. A structured handover note closes it, so the incoming shift starts with full context instead of a blank screen.

A good handover note
  • Open issues and their current status.
  • Anything mid-flight or paused for the next shift.
  • Watch-items — metrics trending the wrong way.
  • Planned maintenance or releases coming up.
  • Who to reach for anything unusual.
A handover that fails
  • “All quiet” — when it wasn't.
  • Open issues left unmentioned.
  • No context on a paused task.
  • Verbal-only, nothing written down.
  • Incoming shift rediscovers problems the hard way.

Key learnings

Document for the non-expert at 3am.

The test of a runbook isn't whether the author understands it — it's whether a tired stand-in can follow it under pressure.

Write the “do not”s.

The dangerous shortcuts deserve as much ink as the steps; naming them prevents the well-meaning mistake.

Handover is part of operations.

Treating the shift handover as a first-class procedure closed the riskiest gap in continuous operations.

📝 Writer's note

An operational playbook is documentation written for its worst-case reader: someone tired, under pressure, who isn't the expert and needs to act now. That constraint shapes everything — short steps, explicit severities, clear escalation, and the “do not” warnings that stop a bad situation getting worse.

The thread connecting this to my release-framework and incident-response work is the same principle: move the thinking earlier. Decide the severity scale, the escalation ladder, and the runbook steps in a calm moment, so the stressful moment is just execution. That's what lets operations scale beyond the people who happen to remember how everything works.
