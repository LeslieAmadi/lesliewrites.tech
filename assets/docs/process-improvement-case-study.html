Process Improvement Case Study

Documenting a process is one skill; improving one is another. This case study walks a full business-process-analysis cycle on a billing-approval workflow mapping the current state, finding the bottleneck and the rework loop hiding inside it, redesigning the future state, and measuring whether the change actually worked.

The business problem

A billing-approval process was “just slow.” Invoices took days to clear, finance and operations blamed each other, and the standard request “can we add more people to it?” would have spent money without fixing the cause. Nobody had actually mapped what the process did, so nobody could see where the time went. “Slow” was a symptom, not a diagnosis.

You can't improve what you haven't mapped

The instinct to “add people” or “work harder” treats the symptom. Process improvement starts by making the current state visible, because the delay is almost never spread evenly it's concentrated in one or two steps, and usually in a rework loop nobody drew. This case study is that diagnosis-before-treatment cycle, done properly.

Current state map what really happens

The first job is an honest map of the process as it actually runs. Drawing it revealed the thing no one had articulated: an approval that bounced back and forth between Finance and the requester every time a detail was missing a rework loop.

Current state. The process looked linear, but a hidden rework loop sent most requests back for missing information sometimes more than once. The delay wasn't in the review step; it was in the round-trips.

🔍

Analysis where the time really went

With the process mapped, the analysis quantified each step's contribution to the total time. The review itself was fast; the round-trips for missing information dominated.

StepShare of total timeFinding
Request submissionSmallFine as-is
Finance reviewSmallFast when info was complete
Rework loop (missing info)Most of itThe real bottleneck
Approval & paymentSmallFine as-is
⚠ The bottleneck wasn't a slow step it was a loop

This is the classic process-analysis insight: people assume the slow part is whichever step feels heavy (here, “Finance review”). The map showed the review was quick; the time vanished into requests bouncing back for missing details. You don't fix that by speeding up review you fix it by stopping the bounce.

💡

Root cause

Why did so many requests arrive incomplete? Walking it back, the cause wasn't careless requesters it was the form.

SymptomApprovals take days
Immediate causeMost requests bounce back for missing information
WhyThe request form didn't ask for everything Finance needed
Root causeThe form was designed around what the requester knew, not what the approver required
Fix the input, not the people

The most powerful process fixes are often upstream and structural. The requesters weren't the problem; they couldn't supply information the form never asked for. Redesigning the intake so it captures what the approver needs the first time dissolves the rework loop at its source.

✔️

Future state the redesign

The redesign added a completeness check at intake the form validates that everything Finance needs is present before the request can be submitted. If the loop can't start, it can't cost days.

Future state. A completeness check at intake means Finance receives only complete requests the rework loop is designed out, and review happens once. The same people, a straighter path.

👤

Measuring the result

An improvement isn't real until it's measured. The redesign defined the metrics up front, so the team could confirm the change worked and watch that it kept working.

MetricWhat it confirms
Rework rate (% sent back)Whether the loop was actually designed out the headline measure
End-to-end approval timeThe outcome the business cared about
First-pass approval rateWhether intake validation is doing its job
Requests per approverThat throughput improved without adding people
Measure the loop, not just the clock

Total time is the metric leadership feels, but rework rate is the one that proves why it improved. Tracking both means that if approval time creeps back up later, the team can tell instantly whether the loop has returned and fix the cause, not the symptom, again.

Key learnings

Map before you fix.

The map turned an emotional “it's slow” into a specific, fixable bottleneck. Diagnosis first, every time.

Look upstream for the cause.

The delay showed up at review, but the cause was the intake form. The best fixes are usually earlier than the symptom.

Measure the mechanism, not just the outcome.

Tracking rework rate not only total time is what makes the improvement durable and diagnosable.

📝 Writer's note

This piece shows the analytical side of process work that pure documentation can hide. The recommendation I received highlighted “the ability to analyse existing processes, identify gaps, and implement practical improvements” this is what that looks like as a method: map honestly, find where time actually goes, trace it to a root cause, redesign, and measure.

The lesson I keep coming back to is that processes rarely fail at the step that feels heavy. They fail in the loops and handoffs between steps the parts no one drew. Making those visible is most of the work; once a rework loop is on the page, the fix is usually obvious. The discipline is resisting the urge to treat the symptom before you've mapped the cause.
