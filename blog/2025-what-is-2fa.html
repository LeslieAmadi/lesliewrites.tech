What Is Two-Factor Authentication And Why Does It Actually Matter?

Most users who do not have two-factor authentication enabled on their accounts have not made a considered decision to leave themselves unprotected. They have encountered a setup process that felt confusing, a benefit explanation that felt abstract, or a recovery scenario that felt frightening. The documentation failed them before the security feature ever had a chance.

In 2024, the Verizon Data Breach Investigations Report found that stolen credentials were involved in the majority of hacking-related breaches. Not sophisticated zero-day exploits. Not nation-state attacks on hardened infrastructure. Stolen usernames and passwords, obtained through phishing campaigns, data breaches at other services, or simple credential stuffing automated tools testing billions of username and password combinations harvested from previous leaks.

The reason this works at scale is that most people reuse passwords. A password leaked in a breach at one service becomes a key that unlocks accounts at dozens of others. The attacker does not need to be particularly skilled or resourceful. They need only a list of leaked credentials and a tool to test them automatically.

Two-factor authentication breaks this attack pattern entirely. It does not matter that an attacker has your password if logging in also requires something they cannot obtain remotely a code generated by an app on your phone, a physical security key sitting on your desk, a biometric that only your body can provide. The stolen credential becomes worthless without the second factor.

This is why 2FA adoption is one of the most high-leverage security interventions available to any organisation. And it is why the documentation that explains, introduces, and walks users through enabling it is not a routine writing task. It is security infrastructure.

Understanding the three factors

Authentication factors are grouped into three categories, and understanding them helps both security teams designing their implementation and technical writers explaining it to users.

  • Something you know a password, PIN, or security question. The most common first factor and the most vulnerable: it can be guessed, observed, phished, or stolen from a database breach.
  • Something you have a mobile phone, authenticator app, hardware security key, or smart card. This factor requires physical possession of a device, which raises the barrier for remote attackers from "obtain a password from a breach" to "physically access a device."
  • Something you are a fingerprint, face scan, voice pattern, or other biometric. Increasingly common on consumer devices and highly resistant to remote attacks, though it introduces considerations around privacy, accessibility, and what happens when a biometric changes or is compromised.

Two-factor authentication combines any two of these categories. The most common implementation combines something you know (a password) with something you have (an authenticator app or hardware key). This is the combination that protects against the credential stuffing attacks that account for the majority of real-world breaches.

The four main 2FA methods: an honest comparison

Not all 2FA implementations provide the same level of protection, and the choice between them involves genuine trade-offs between security strength and usability. Understanding those trade-offs is essential for technical writers documenting 2FA because users who understand why one method is stronger than another are more likely to choose the stronger method.

SMS codes

A one-time code sent by text message. Widely supported, requires no additional app installation. Vulnerable to SIM-swapping attacks, in which an attacker convinces a mobile carrier to transfer a phone number to a new SIM card they control.

Good

Authenticator apps

Apps like Google Authenticator, Authy, or 1Password generate time-based one-time passwords (TOTP) that change every 30 seconds. Not vulnerable to SIM-swapping. Codes cannot be intercepted in transit because they are generated locally on the device.

Better

Push notifications

An app sends a push notification asking the user to approve or deny a login attempt. Convenient and phishing-resistant when users verify the login context before approving. Vulnerable to MFA fatigue attacks, where attackers send repeated requests until a tired user approves.

Better

Hardware security keys

Physical devices using public-key cryptography. The key signs a challenge from the server during login the private key never leaves the device and cannot be phished remotely. Google's internal research found hardware keys block 100% of automated phishing attacks.

Strongest

For most consumer applications, authenticator apps provide an excellent balance of security and usability. SMS is substantially better than no 2FA and is appropriate for lower-risk accounts or contexts where users are unlikely to be targeted by sophisticated attacks. For high-value accounts administrative access, financial systems, developer credentials hardware keys are worth the additional cost and friction.

Why users do not enable 2FA and what documentation can do about it

Security teams often frame low 2FA adoption as a behaviour problem users making poor security choices. The research suggests it is more often a communication problem. Users who understand what 2FA protects against, who have had a smooth setup experience, and who are confident they can recover access if they lose their device adopt 2FA at significantly higher rates.

The three most common reasons users give for not enabling 2FA are instructive for technical writers:

  • "I didn't understand what it was or why I needed it." The benefit was not communicated specifically enough. "Adds an extra layer of security" is not a benefit it is a category. "Keeps your account safe even if someone obtains your password" is a benefit.
  • "The setup seemed too complicated." The setup documentation had too many steps, too many options, or too much explanatory text between the user and the first moment of success.
  • "I was worried about getting locked out." The recovery process was not explained at the point where 2FA was being enabled. Users imagined losing access to their phone and having no way back into their account and they were right to worry, because the documentation had not addressed it.

Each of these is a documentation failure with a documentation solution.

Writing 2FA documentation that actually changes behaviour

Good 2FA documentation answers four questions before the user has to ask them. Answering all four clearly, specifically, and in the right order is the difference between documentation that users read and forget and documentation that results in 2FA being enabled.

  1. Why should I enable this? Be specific about the threat. "2FA means your account stays protected even if your password is leaked in a breach at another service you use" is specific. It describes a real, recognisable scenario. It makes the threat concrete rather than abstract.
  2. What will change about my login experience? Set accurate expectations before the user enables 2FA, not after. Walk through a typical login flow: enter your password, open your authenticator app, enter the six-digit code. A user who knows exactly what will happen is not surprised when it does.
  3. What if I lose access to my second factor? This is the anxiety that stops many users from enabling 2FA. Address it directly and early. Document backup codes, alternative recovery methods, and the account recovery process. A user who knows they have a safety net will use the feature.
  4. How do I set it up? Numbered steps. Clear navigation paths. Realistic screenshots if the interface is complex. A specific statement of what success looks like at the end of the process.

A model 2FA setup guide

Here is what this looks like when it is written well. Notice the specificity throughout no hedging, no vagueness, no assumed knowledge.

Example: 2FA setup documentation

Protect your account with two-factor authentication

Two-factor authentication means that even if someone has your password, they still cannot access your account without your phone. It takes about two minutes to set up.

You will need: An authenticator app Google Authenticator, Authy, or 1Password all work. If you do not have one, install it before you start.

1. Go to Account Settings → Security → Two-Factor Authentication
2. Click Enable 2FA
3. Open your authenticator app and scan the QR code shown on screen
4. Enter the six-digit code your app generates it changes every 30 seconds, so use the current one
5. Save your backup codes somewhere secure a password manager is ideal. You will need these if you ever lose access to your phone.

What logging in will look like from now on: After entering your password, you will be asked for a six-digit code from your authenticator app. The code changes every 30 seconds always use the current one.

Every element of this guide is deliberate. The benefit is stated specifically in the first sentence. The time investment is honest two minutes, not "just a few steps." The prerequisite is clear and actionable. The backup codes are addressed explicitly, with a specific recommendation. The post-setup experience is described so there are no surprises.

On backup codes: the most important thing most documentation skips

Backup codes single-use recovery codes generated at the time of 2FA setup are the safety net that makes 2FA adoption feel safe rather than frightening. A user who knows they have backup codes stored securely is a user who can enable 2FA without anxiety. A user who was never told about backup codes, or who did not save them, is a user who will eventually be locked out of their account and will blame the documentation correctly for not making their importance clear.

Backup codes deserve prominent documentation at the moment of setup, not a footnote in an FAQ. They deserve a specific recommendation for where to store them. They deserve an explanation of what to do when you have used them all. This is not a peripheral concern for many users, it is the thing that stands between them and enabling a security feature that would meaningfully protect them.

Security documentation that respects users' time, speaks honestly about threats, and addresses genuine anxieties does not just inform it changes behaviour. That is the standard worth writing to.
