Writing Model Cards That Matter

Most model cards fail before anyone reads them. They exist to satisfy a checklist, not to communicate. Here's how to write ones that engineers, product teams, and regulators can actually rely on.

Model cards were introduced by Google researchers in 2019 as a framework for transparent AI documentation. The idea was simple: every deployed model should come with a structured document explaining what it does, how it was trained, where it works well, and crucially, where it does not. Six years later, model cards are widely adopted and widely misused.

The problem is not that teams don't write them. Most do. The problem is that the resulting documents read like legal disclaimers: dense, defensive, and written for no particular reader. They describe a model the way a warranty card describes a toaster. Nobody reads them. Nobody trusts them. And when something goes wrong in production, nobody can find the information they needed.

Good model card documentation is a different discipline entirely. It requires the same skills as any serious technical writing understanding your audience, making deliberate structural choices, and being honest about what you do and do not know. This guide is about doing that well.

Why most model cards fail

Before writing better model cards, it helps to understand why existing ones tend to fall short. There are three failure modes that appear again and again.

They are written after the fact. Documentation produced at the end of a development cycle reflects what the team remembers, not what actually happened. Evaluation decisions, dataset choices, and known limitations get summarised or quietly omitted because the people who made those decisions have moved on to the next project.

They have no clear audience. A model card written for a compliance officer looks very different from one written for an integrating engineer. When the author has not decided who they are writing for, the document tries to serve everyone and ends up serving no one. The language is simultaneously too technical for a non-specialist and too vague for someone who actually needs to implement the system.

They confuse description with communication. Listing an accuracy figure is description. Explaining what that figure means for a specific use case, in plain language, with context about the evaluation conditions that is communication. Most model cards do the former and call it done.

The core principle

A model card is not a technical specification. It is a communication document. Its job is to give a specific reader the information they need to make a confident, informed decision about whether and how to use this model.

Know your reader before you write a word

The most important decision you make when writing a model card is choosing your primary reader. In practice, model cards are read by several different audiences and each needs something different from the document.

Who reads model cards and what they need

  • Engineers integrating the model Input/output formats, latency characteristics, API behaviour, known edge cases, and what happens when the model fails or returns low-confidence results.
  • Product managers Intended use cases, out-of-scope uses, performance across relevant user segments, and the risk profile of deployment decisions.
  • Compliance and legal teams Training data provenance, evaluation methodology, bias testing results, and documentation of known limitations and mitigations.
  • External researchers and auditors Reproducibility information, dataset details, evaluation benchmarks, and honest assessment of the model's limitations.
  • End users (where applicable) What the model is designed to do, what it is not designed to do, and how to interpret its outputs.

Most model cards will have a primary audience and several secondary ones. Write for the primary reader first, then make sure the document is structured so secondary readers can navigate to what they need without wading through content that is not relevant to them.

The sections that actually matter

The original model card framework proposed by Mitchell et al. included nine sections. Many organisations have adapted this into their own templates. What follows is not a rigid prescription it is a description of what good model card documentation covers, and why each element matters.

Model overview

One paragraph. What does this model do, in plain language? What problem was it built to solve? Who built it and when? This section should be readable by someone with no technical background in under thirty seconds. If it takes longer, rewrite it.

Intended use

Be specific about what this model is designed to do, and equally specific about what it is not. The out-of-scope uses section is not a legal formality it is often the most useful part of the document for engineers and product teams trying to make deployment decisions. Vague language here causes real harm downstream.

❌ Vague

"This model should not be used for high-stakes decisions."

✅ Specific

"This model is not validated for use in medical diagnosis, credit scoring, or any context where an incorrect prediction could result in harm to an individual."

Training data

Where did the training data come from? How was it collected, filtered, and preprocessed? What is known about its demographic composition? What is not known? This section requires honesty about gaps. A model card that claims comprehensive data documentation while glossing over unknown provenance is not transparent it is theatre.

Evaluation results

Raw performance metrics without context are nearly useless. An accuracy figure means nothing without knowing what dataset it was measured on, what the baseline was, and how performance varies across different subgroups, languages, or conditions. Always include disaggregated evaluation results where possible.

❌ Context-free

"The model achieves 94.2% accuracy on the test set."

✅ Contextualised

"The model achieves 94.2% accuracy on the internal test set (n=12,000, English-language only). Accuracy drops to 81.3% on the multilingual validation set. Performance on low-resource languages has not been evaluated."

Limitations and risks

This is the section most authors either skip or sanitise into meaninglessness. It is also the section that matters most when something goes wrong. Write it as if you are briefing a colleague who is about to deploy this model in production for the first time. What do they need to know? What should they watch for? What has already gone wrong in testing that they should be aware of?

A useful test

Read your limitations section and ask: if this model caused harm in production, would this document have given a reasonable engineer enough information to anticipate that risk? If the answer is no, the section is not finished.

Ethical considerations

Describe the ethical review process if one was conducted. Identify any fairness concerns that were raised and how they were addressed or note honestly that they remain open. This section should not read like a public relations statement. It should read like a genuine accounting of the decisions that were made and the values that guided them.

Writing style that actually works

Model cards suffer from the same writing problems as most technical documentation: passive voice, hedge-laden language, and a tendency to describe what was done rather than what it means. A few concrete principles help.

Use active voice for decisions and passive voice for processes. "We excluded data from users under 18" is clearer than "Data from users under 18 was excluded." The active voice makes clear that a human made a deliberate choice which matters for accountability.

Define every metric the first time you use it. Not every reader knows what F1 score measures or why precision matters more than recall in a particular context. One sentence of context transforms a number into information.

Write limitations as if you mean them. "The model may occasionally produce unexpected results" tells the reader nothing. "The model consistently underperforms on text containing domain-specific medical terminology, with error rates approximately 3x higher than the overall average" tells them something they can act on.

Keep the overview short and the detail rich. The first section should be readable in under a minute. Everything after it can go deep because readers who need that depth will look for it, and readers who do not can stop reading.

Model cards in practice

The best model cards are living documents. They are updated when the model is retrained, when new evaluation results become available, or when a deployment surfaces a previously unknown limitation. Building that update process into your documentation workflow from the start rather than treating the model card as a one-time deliverable is what separates documentation that remains useful from documentation that quietly goes stale.

If you are working with a team that has never written model cards before, start with the three sections that deliver the most immediate value: intended use, evaluation results, and limitations. A model card that covers those three things honestly is more useful than a complete nine-section document where every field contains a placeholder or a hedge.

The goal is not compliance. The goal is communication giving the people who will build with, deploy, and be affected by this model the information they need to do that responsibly. That is what makes a model card matter.
