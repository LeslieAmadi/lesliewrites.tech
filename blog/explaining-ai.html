Explaining AI Clearly: A Technical Writer's Perspective

AI systems are reshaping how we work, make decisions, and understand the world. The writers who can explain them clearly accurately, honestly, and without condescension are doing some of the most important communication work of this decade. Most are not doing it well enough. Here is how to do it better.

I want to start with something that might sound counterintuitive: the problem with most AI writing is not that it is too technical. It is that it is not honest enough.

We have an abundance of content about artificial intelligence that confidently oversimplifies, that uses analogies which break down the moment someone thinks about them seriously, that describes what AI systems do without acknowledging what they do not do, what they get wrong, or what nobody yet fully understands about why they behave the way they do. This content prioritises comprehensibility over accuracy in a way that ultimately serves neither because a reader who later discovers that the simplified version was misleading does not just update their mental model. They lose trust in the source that misled them.

At the same time, we have technical documentation about AI systems that is precise, thorough, and essentially unreadable by anyone who did not write it. Research papers, model specifications, system architecture documents these are accurate but inaccessible, which means their accuracy does not help the people who most need the information they contain.

Good AI writing lives in neither of these places. It lives in the uncomfortable space between them accurate enough to be trusted, clear enough to be useful, honest enough to acknowledge what is unknown, and human enough to care about the reader who is trying to make sense of a genuinely complex and consequential technology.

This piece is about how to write that way.

Why AI writing is genuinely hard

Before talking about craft, it is worth being honest about the difficulty of the subject matter because the challenges of explaining AI clearly are not just communication challenges. They are epistemic challenges. AI systems, and especially modern large language models and deep learning systems, are not fully understood even by the researchers who build them. We can describe their inputs and outputs. We can measure their performance on specific benchmarks. We can identify failure modes empirically. But explaining exactly why a neural network produces a given output, in terms that are both accurate and human-comprehensible, remains an active research problem. Explainability and interpretability are entire subfields of AI research precisely because this question is genuinely hard.

A technical writer approaching AI documentation needs to hold two things simultaneously: the obligation to be accurate about what is known, and the obligation to be honest about what is not. Both require specific techniques. Both require a different relationship with certainty than most writing demands.

The foundational principle

Clarity and honesty are not in tension in AI writing they reinforce each other. A document that accurately describes the limits of an AI system is more useful, more trustworthy, and ultimately more readable than one that overstates its capabilities. The reader who understands what a system cannot do is equipped to use it responsibly. The reader who has been given an inflated picture of its capabilities will eventually discover the gap at a cost.

Step 1 Know what kind of AI you are writing about

"AI" is not one thing. The term covers a spectrum of technologies with fundamentally different architectures, capabilities, and limitations. A writer who conflates a rule-based recommendation system with a large language model, or who describes a computer vision model using language borrowed from cognitive science, produces documentation that is technically inaccurate before it is even finished.

Before writing anything, establish which category of AI system you are documenting. The distinctions matter to the reader, and to the accuracy of what you write.

A practical taxonomy for AI writers

  • Rule-based systems Explicit decision trees or logic rules written by humans. Predictable and auditable the system does exactly what its rules specify. Not "learning" in any meaningful sense. Common in fraud detection and content moderation. Easy to explain; limitations are the rules themselves.
  • Classical machine learning Models that learn statistical patterns from labelled data regression, decision trees, random forests, SVMs. Outputs can often be explained in terms of which features influenced the prediction. Used in credit scoring, spam filtering, medical risk stratification.
  • Deep learning / neural networks Multi-layer networks that learn hierarchical representations. Highly capable on complex tasks (image recognition, speech). Notoriously difficult to interpret the path from input to output passes through hundreds of millions of learned parameters whose individual contributions are opaque.
  • Large language models (LLMs) Transformer-based models trained on massive text corpora. Produce fluent, contextually appropriate text. Do not "understand" in any sense equivalent to human understanding. Prone to confident hallucination. Behaviour is probabilistic and context-dependent in ways that resist deterministic explanation.
  • Multimodal systems Systems that process and generate across multiple modalities text, images, audio, video. Combine capabilities and limitations from multiple model types. Documentation must address each modality's specific failure modes.

Each of these requires different language, different analogies, different caveats, and different honesty about limitations. Writing about a decision tree as if it were a neural network, or describing a language model as if it "knows" things in the way a database knows facts, produces fundamental misunderstanding that no amount of clear prose can compensate for.

Step 2 Start with consequences, not components

The most common structural mistake in AI documentation is beginning with the system how it works, what it is made of, how it was trained before establishing why any of that matters to the person reading. Readers do not have an intrinsic interest in backpropagation or attention mechanisms. They have an interest in decisions that will be made about them, tasks they need to complete, risks they need to understand, or capabilities they want to use. Begin there.

This is not the same as burying the technical detail. The technical detail matters and it belongs in the documentation. But the structural question is what comes first, what establishes the frame through which everything else is understood.

❌ System-first loses the reader before they start

"This system uses a convolutional neural network trained on 2.3 million labelled chest X-rays using a ResNet-50 architecture with transfer learning from ImageNet. The model outputs a probability score between 0 and 1 for each of 14 pathological conditions."

✅ Consequence-first earns the technical explanation

"This system helps radiologists identify signs of 14 different chest conditions in X-rays including pneumonia, cardiomegaly, and pleural effusion. It flags cases for priority review and provides a confidence score for each finding. Here is how it works, and what its limitations are."

The second version earns its technical explanation. The reader now has a reason to understand how the system works, because they understand what it does and why it matters. The architecture details that follow land in a context that makes them meaningful rather than abstract.

Step 3 Use analogies carefully, and know when to retire them

Analogies are the primary tool of AI explanation. They are also one of the most commonly misused. A good analogy gives a reader a foothold it maps an unfamiliar concept onto something they already understand well enough to get started. A bad analogy creates a mental model that the reader will have to actively unlearn later.

The most important thing to understand about analogies in AI writing is that all of them break down. There is no perfect analogy for a neural network, because there is nothing in ordinary human experience that works the way a neural network works. The question is not "which analogy is correct?" none of them are correct. The question is "which analogy is useful at this level of explanation, and where does it break down?"

When you use an analogy, acknowledge its limits. This is not a weakness in the explanation it is what distinguishes an honest explanation from a misleading simplification.

❌ Analogy without limits creates a false mental model

"A neural network learns the same way a child learns by seeing lots of examples and gradually understanding the patterns."

This implies understanding, generalisation, and a learning process similar to human cognition. None of these comparisons hold under scrutiny.

✅ Analogy with acknowledged limits builds accurate intuition

"Training a neural network is sometimes compared to learning from examples the system adjusts its internal parameters each time it makes a mistake, gradually improving its predictions. Unlike human learning, this process has no understanding of what the examples mean it is purely statistical pattern-matching. The analogy is useful for understanding the training process but breaks down when applied to how the model 'thinks.'"

Step 4 Write about uncertainty as a feature, not a bug

AI systems are probabilistic. They do not know things they assign probabilities. They do not make decisions they produce outputs that influence decisions. They do not understand context they identify patterns in data that correlate with context. These distinctions are not pedantic. They have real consequences for how the systems should be used, trusted, and governed.

Most AI writing avoids this language because it feels like it undermines confidence in the technology. In practice, the opposite is true. Readers who understand that an AI system produces a confidence score of 0.73 for a given prediction, and who understand what that means, are better positioned to use that output appropriately than readers who have been told the system "identifies" or "detects" something — language that implies a certainty the system does not actually have.

Writing about probability and uncertainty clearly requires specific vocabulary choices. Here is a reference I use when reviewing AI documentation:

Language that respects probabilistic reality

  • Instead of "the model knows" Write "the model assigns a high probability to" or "the model's output indicates" knowledge implies understanding that these systems do not possess.
  • Instead of "the model detected" Write "the model flagged" or "the model predicted" detected implies certainty that a probabilistic output cannot provide.
  • Instead of "the AI decided" Write "the system's output was used to inform a decision" AI systems produce outputs; humans (or other systems) make decisions based on them.
  • Instead of "the model understands" Write "the model identifies patterns consistent with" understanding implies semantic comprehension that current AI systems do not have.
  • Instead of "accurate" Specify: accurate on what dataset, measured how, compared to what baseline. Accuracy without context is meaningless.

Step 5 Document what the system gets wrong

This is the section that most AI documentation either omits or buries in a footnote. It is also the most important section for any reader who is going to use the system in a real-world context.

Every AI system has failure modes. Every model trained on real-world data inherits the biases and gaps in that data. Every system performs differently across different demographic groups, languages, geographic regions, lighting conditions, input formats, and edge cases. The question is not whether your system has these characteristics it does. The question is whether you document them.

Documentation that describes only the successes of a system is not honest documentation. It is marketing copy with a different typeface. And it causes real harm because readers who are not warned about failure modes will discover them in deployment, at a cost they could not have anticipated.

What failure documentation should cover

At minimum, every AI system documentation should address: known performance degradation by demographic group, language, or input type; the conditions under which the model is most likely to produce incorrect outputs; the failure modes that carry the highest risk if they occur undetected; and what the system was not designed to do, expressed specifically enough that a real decision-maker could use that information.

Step 6 Layer complexity intentionally

AI concepts have depth. Most readers approach them from different starting points. The layering structure of your documentation determines whether readers at each level can get what they need without being bored by what they already know or lost in what they are not yet ready for.

A layered explanation moves from the concrete and human-relevant toward the technical and abstract and it does this explicitly, signposting each layer so readers can decide how deep to go.

Example: Explaining transformer attention three layers

Layer 1 — Plain language (no assumptions):
"When processing a sentence, this model considers every word in relation to every other word not just the words immediately adjacent to it. This helps it understand that 'bank' in 'river bank' means something different from 'bank' in 'savings bank.'"

Layer 2 — Conceptual (assumes basic familiarity):
"The attention mechanism assigns a weight to each word in the input sequence relative to every other word. These weights determine how much influence each word has on the model's interpretation of each other word. The weights are learned during training and vary by context."

Layer 3 — Technical (for practitioners):
"Multi-head self-attention computes attention weights as softmax(QKT/√dk)V, where Q, K, and V are learned projections of the input. Multiple attention heads allow the model to attend to different aspects of the input simultaneously, with outputs concatenated and linearly projected."

Each layer is accurate. Each builds on the last without contradicting it. A reader can stop at layer one and have a useful mental model. A reader who needs layer three can get there without finding the journey condescending.

Step 7 — Write about AI governance and responsible use

In 2025, no piece of AI documentation is complete without addressing the governance context in which the system operates. This is not optional it is now a regulatory and ethical expectation in many jurisdictions, and an increasingly significant factor in procurement decisions across industries.

The EU AI Act, which entered into force in August 2024, establishes risk categories for AI systems and corresponding documentation requirements. Systems classified as high-risk including those used in healthcare, education, employment, critical infrastructure, and law enforcement face mandatory requirements for technical documentation, human oversight provisions, transparency to users, and conformity assessments. Technical writers working on AI systems in regulated industries need to understand these requirements, because the documentation they produce is part of the compliance evidence.

Beyond regulatory compliance, good governance documentation addresses the questions that determine whether an AI system should be deployed at all and whether the people affected by it have adequate recourse when it affects them adversely.

Governance questions that belong in AI documentation

  • Who is this system for? Intended users, beneficiaries, and affected parties including those who may be affected without having chosen to interact with the system.
  • Who reviewed this system? The ethical review process, fairness testing methodology, and the people and teams responsible for ongoing monitoring.
  • What decisions does it inform? The specific decisions that will be made using this system's outputs, and the human roles in that decision-making process.
  • What recourse exists? How affected individuals can challenge or appeal decisions that were informed by the system's outputs.
  • How is it monitored? The metrics tracked in production, the thresholds that trigger review, and who is responsible for acting on monitoring signals.

Step 8 — Anticipate the future reader

AI documentation has a shelf life problem. The systems evolve. The models are retrained. The regulatory landscape shifts. The research that informed a design decision gets updated or contradicted. Documentation written today may be read and used to make decisions in a context significantly different from the one in which it was written.

Good AI documentation anticipates this by being explicit about the temporal context of its claims. When was this system evaluated? On what version? Against what benchmark? With what dataset composition? These are not bureaucratic details they are the information that allows a future reader to assess whether the documentation is still valid for their use case.

Equally important: build review cycles into the documentation itself. A model card or system documentation that includes a "last reviewed" date and a designated reviewer is a document that has a mechanism for staying current. One that does not is a document that will quietly become misleading over time, without anyone necessarily noticing.

What this looks like in practice: a real-world scenario

To ground everything above in something concrete, consider a hiring tool an AI system that screens job applications and ranks candidates for initial review. This is a real and controversial category of AI deployment, with documented cases of bias and harm. It is also a category where good documentation makes a meaningful difference.

A poorly documented hiring tool might describe itself as "an objective, data-driven screening system that improves hiring efficiency." This is not technically false, but it is deeply misleading. It implies objectivity that probabilistic systems trained on historical data cannot have. It implies efficiency without acknowledging the cost in equity, in opportunity, in human dignity when the system makes errors that disadvantage protected groups.

A well-documented hiring tool would instead describe its training data provenance where the historical hiring decisions came from, what bias they may encode, and how that bias was tested for and mitigated. It would describe its performance disaggregated by gender, ethnicity, age, and disability status not just its aggregate accuracy. It would describe the human review process that follows its outputs and the mechanism by which candidates can flag concerns. It would describe its limitations explicitly the kinds of candidates and roles for which the system performs least reliably.

That documentation takes longer to write. It requires access to information that organisations sometimes prefer not to make explicit. It requires the technical writer to ask uncomfortable questions and to resist pressure to present the system more favourably than the evidence supports.

It is also the documentation that actually serves the people who rely on it the HR teams using the system, the candidates affected by it, and the organisations that will eventually be accountable for its outcomes.

The future of AI documentation

We are in the early stages of what will be, I think, a significant professionalisation of AI documentation as a discipline. Several forces are driving this:

Regulatory pressure is increasing globally. The EU AI Act is the most comprehensive framework currently in force, but similar legislation is advancing in the UK, US, Canada, and across Asia-Pacific. Each framework creates documentation requirements — and penalties for non-compliance — that make good AI documentation a legal necessity rather than an optional best practice.

Procurement standards are tightening. Large organisations governments, enterprises, healthcare systems are increasingly requiring detailed technical documentation as part of AI procurement. Systems that cannot produce adequate documentation are being passed over for systems that can.

Public accountability is growing. Investigative journalism, academic research, and civil society organisations are increasingly scrutinising AI systems in deployment and finding cases where the gap between documented capabilities and actual behaviour is significant. Each case damages trust in AI systems broadly and increases pressure for more rigorous and honest documentation.

The technical writer who can produce documentation that meets these emerging standards accurate, honest, layered, human-centred, governance-aware, and built to last is not just filling a communication gap. They are contributing to the infrastructure of trustworthy AI deployment. That is a significant and consequential role.

It requires more than good writing. It requires technical literacy not to the level of an AI researcher, but enough to ask the right questions and evaluate the answers critically. It requires ethical grounding the ability to recognise when documentation is being used to obscure rather than illuminate, and the willingness to push back. And it requires a genuine commitment to the reader to the engineer using the system, the patient affected by it, the regulator evaluating it, and the public ultimately living with its consequences.

Those readers deserve writing that takes them seriously. That is what explaining AI clearly actually means.
