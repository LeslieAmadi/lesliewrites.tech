SEO Writing for Humans, Not Algorithms

For most of the 2010s, SEO writing meant something specific and teachable: identify the keywords, hit the density targets, structure the headings correctly, and watch the rankings follow. It worked, to a degree, because the systems being gamed were simpler. Then the systems got smarter, and everything that had been working started working against the people doing it.

I want to be honest about something before we go further. The phrase "SEO writing" makes some writers uncomfortable, and not without reason. It has historically been associated with content that prioritises discoverability over quality articles padded to hit a word count, repetitive keyword insertion that reads unnaturally, topics chosen for search volume rather than genuine value to the reader. Some of what has been published under the SEO writing banner deserves that reputation.

But that era is substantially over, not because organisations decided to be more ethical, but because search engines got better at identifying and demoting content written for machines rather than people. Google's Helpful Content Update, rolled out through 2022 and 2023, was an explicit signal: content that exists primarily to rank, rather than to inform, will be penalised. The algorithm has been getting increasingly effective at distinguishing between the two.

What has replaced the old approach is, in most respects, just good writing writing that understands its audience, answers genuine questions clearly, and earns its place in search results by being useful. The SEO layer, applied well, amplifies that usefulness. Applied badly, it undermines it. Understanding the difference is the whole job.

Search intent: the concept that changes everything

The most important shift in how we think about SEO writing in the past decade is the concept of search intent the actual goal a person has when they type something into a search engine. Keywords are a proxy for intent, not a description of it. The same keyword can represent completely different needs depending on context, and content that serves one need almost certainly fails the others.

"API documentation" might be searched by a developer looking for reference material for a specific API they are integrating. It might be searched by a technical writer looking for best practices and templates. It might be searched by a product manager trying to understand what good API documentation looks like before approving a budget. All three will type similar or identical queries. None of them want the same content.

The question to ask before writing any piece of content with search visibility in mind is not "what keyword am I targeting?" It is "who is searching for this, and what do they actually need?" The answer to that question determines structure, depth, format, and tone and once you know it, the keyword question largely answers itself, because you will naturally use the language your audience uses to describe their problem.

The four categories of search intent

  • Informational The person wants to understand something. "How does two-factor authentication work?" "What is a REST API?" "Why are my API calls returning 401?" The content should explain clearly, completely, and without assuming more background knowledge than the query implies.
  • Navigational The person is trying to reach a specific resource. "Stripe API documentation." "GitHub Actions syntax reference." SEO is largely irrelevant here they want the resource, and if you have it, they will find it. If you do not, no amount of optimisation will help.
  • Commercial The person is evaluating options. "Best API documentation tools." "Docusaurus vs ReadMe comparison." "Technical writing software review." Content should compare options honestly, acknowledge trade-offs, and help the reader make an informed decision. Content that serves only one side of a comparison reads as promotional and will be treated as such.
  • Transactional The person is ready to act. "Download Postman." "Sign up for API access." "Buy technical writing course." Content should facilitate the action efficiently. This is not the moment for extensive explanatory prose it is the moment for clear, frictionless conversion.

Writing a 2,000-word informational piece for a transactional query is a mismatch. So is a brief overview for a query where someone needs a comprehensive guide. Matching content depth and format to search intent is more impactful than any keyword density optimisation technique.

The honest truth about keywords

Keyword research has genuine value. Understanding the language your audience uses to search for solutions to the problems you can help with tells you what to write about and how to frame it. Finding that "API documentation template" gets substantially more searches than "API docs boilerplate" tells you something useful about how your audience describes what they need.

What keyword research does not tell you is how often to use those keywords in a given piece, or that including them more frequently will improve your rankings. That model of SEO the keyword density model does not reflect how modern search algorithms work. They evaluate semantic relevance, the depth and quality of coverage of a topic, the engagement signals from users who click through and read, and whether the content appears to have been written for humans or for crawlers.

A useful reframe: think of keywords not as ingredients to add to content in measured amounts, but as a vocabulary check. A well-written piece about API documentation best practices will naturally contain the language developers and technical writers use when searching for help with API documentation because it was written by someone who understands the subject and the audience. The keywords appear because the content is relevant, not the other way around.

❌ Keyword stuffing serves the algorithm, not the reader

"API documentation is important for any API. Good API documentation helps developers understand your API. Our API documentation guide covers API documentation examples, API documentation templates, and API documentation best practices."

✅ Natural language serves the reader, ranks as a result

"Good developer documentation gets engineers to their first successful API call in under five minutes. The difference is almost always in the examples: realistic test values instead of placeholders, complete responses alongside the requests, and error cases that show developers what to expect when things go wrong."

Structure: writing for scanners and readers simultaneously

Most people who land on a piece of content through search scan it before they read it. They are making a rapid assessment: does this page appear to answer the question I searched for? Is it going to be worth the time investment? Research on reading behaviour consistently shows that the majority of users spend the first few seconds scanning headings and the first sentence of each paragraph before deciding whether to commit to reading in full.

This has practical implications for how technical content should be structured. The most important information should appear early in the title, in the opening paragraph, in the first heading. Headings should describe the content beneath them, not just label it. "What makes a great API example?" is a useful heading. "API Examples" is not. A user scanning the page can evaluate the first; the second tells them nothing.

Short paragraphs are not a stylistic choice they are a readability choice. A wall of unbroken text signals effort and discourages continuation. Paragraph breaks, at intervals that reflect genuine shifts in thought, make content approachable. So do lists, when the content is genuinely list-like. Lists used to break up prose that would read better as connected sentences are not easier to read they are just visually different, and often harder to follow because the connective tissue between ideas has been removed.

The experience after the click

Search engines measure more than keyword relevance. They measure what happens after someone clicks a result how long they stay, whether they click back quickly (a signal that the content did not answer their question), whether they scroll through the page or abandon it after two paragraphs. These signals are not definitive ranking factors in the way that links or structured data are, but they are part of a broader picture of content quality that modern search systems evaluate.

The practical implication is that content which genuinely serves its reader that answers the question clearly, provides enough depth to be useful, and does not waste the reader's time with padding will generate better engagement signals than content that ranks well through optimisation but fails to deliver on the implicit promise of the search result. Optimise for the experience after the click, and the ranking tends to follow.

A practical SEO checklist for technical writers

  • Title tag matches the search intent The title should reflect what the page actually delivers, using the language the target reader would use to search for it.
  • Meta description answers "why click this result?" Under 160 characters, specific enough to differentiate from other results, honest about what the reader will find.
  • The question is answered in the first 100 words Do not make readers scroll to discover whether this page has what they came for.
  • Headings describe, not just label Every H2 and H3 should tell a skimmer something useful about what comes next.
  • Internal links connect related content Link to related articles and documentation where relevant. It serves readers and signals topical depth.
  • Alt text on images is descriptive and specific Not "figure1.png." Not keyword-stuffed. An accurate description of what the image shows, in plain language.
  • Images are compressed Large unoptimised images are the most common cause of slow page loads. Slow pages lose readers before they start.
  • The content actually answers the question it promises to answer This should not need to be on the checklist. It consistently does.

None of these elements replace good writing. They are the final verification that good writing is findable and that it delivers on what it promises. That is, when you think about it, the same thing technical documentation should always do be genuinely useful to the person who needs it, at the moment they need it, in a form they can act on.
